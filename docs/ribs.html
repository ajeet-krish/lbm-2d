Ribbed Channel Flow

Turbulent flow over repeated transverse ribs on a channel wall. This configuration models internal cooling passages in gas turbine blades, heat exchanger channels, and electronics micro-channel heat sinks. The ribs enhance heat transfer by tripping the boundary layer and creating separated flow regions that reattach between ribs.

Engineering Significance

Gas turbine blade cooling: Rib turbulators are cast into internal cooling passages of high-pressure turbine blades. The ribs augment the heat transfer coefficient by 2-4x over a smooth channel at the cost of a 3-5x increase in friction factor. Optimising rib geometry (height, pitch, angle) is a key design problem.

Heat exchangers: Compact heat exchangers use ribbed/ dimpled surfaces to increase the surface area and disrupt the thermal boundary layer. The friction factor is a direct input to the pumping power requirement.

Electronics cooling: Micro-channel heat sinks with rib-like obstructions improve cooling of high-power-density electronics. The pressure drop penalty must be balanced against the thermal performance.

Friction Factor

The friction factor is computed from the body force required to drive the flow through the ribbed channel at steady state:

f = \frac{2 \; D_h \; \tau_{\text{wall}}}{\rho \; u_{\text{bulk}}^2} = \frac{2 \; F_{\text{body}} \; H^2}{u_{\text{bulk}}^2}

where Fbody is the applied body force (momentum source per cell), H = NY is the channel height in lattice units, and ubulk is the bulk velocity averaged across the channel at the final time step.

For a smooth channel in laminar flow, the theoretical Poiseuille friction factor is fsmooth = 64 / Re (Blasius for turbulent: f = 0.0791 / Re1/4). The ribbed channel friction factor is compared against the smooth-channel baseline at the same Re.

Reattachment Length

After each rib, the flow separates and forms a recirculation bubble. The reattachment length Xr is the distance from the rib trailing edge to the point where the wall shear stress changes sign. It is measured in the solver by scanning the wall-tangent velocity along the bottom wall between ribs.

Discussion

The ribbed channel at LBM-relevant Re values (50-200) is laminar, while real turbine cooling passages operate at Re = 10,000-100,000 with fully turbulent flow. The laminar simulations capture the correct qualitative trends: increasing Xr/h with Re, and elevated friction factor relative to smooth channel. A future extension with a turbulence model (Smagorinsky LES) would enable quantitative comparison to engineering correlations (e.g., Han 1988 for ribbed channel friction and heat transfer).

The obstacle forces from each rib are extracted via the momentum exchange method, and the body force driving the channel is adjusted to maintain the prescribed Reynolds number. The friction factor computation uses the final-state bulk velocity and total obstacle drag to infer the wall shear stress equivalent.
