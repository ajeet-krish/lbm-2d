Periodic Hills: Canonical Separated Turbulent Flow

Flow over periodic hills is a canonical benchmark for separated turbulent flows (Moser, Kim & Moin 1993). The sinusoidal hill profile induces a separation bubble, recirculation zone, and reattachment, ideal for validating LES turbulence models. The Smagorinsky subgrid-scale model is essential here (auto-LES activates when tau < 0.55).

Three sinusoidal hill-valley periods are tiled across the domain, each of wavelength NX/3, and the periodic x-boundary connects identical hill heights. The reduced hill height (hmax = H/6) keeps the channel open so the full sinusoidal terrain and its separation bubble are clearly visible in the viewer.

Physics

The sinusoidal hill profile is defined as: yhill(x) = H - hmax * [1 - cos(2*pi*3*x/NX)]2 / 2 (three hill periods across the domain, each of wavelength NX/3). Flow accelerates over the hill crest, separates at the summit, and forms a recirculation bubble in the valley. Reattachment occurs downstream, and the flow re-accelerates over the next hill.

At Re = 1000, transitional effects appear with unsteady separation. At Re = 2800 (DNS reference), the flow is fully turbulent with Reynolds stresses dominating momentum transport.

Validation

1000 Transitional Hill crest ~0.3L downstream Unsteady, LES required
2800 Turbulent Hill crest ~0.2L downstream LES benchmark (Moser 1993)
This is a demanding LES test case. The Smagorinsky model (Cs = 0.12) provides subgrid-scale eddy viscosity to stabilize the high-Re simulations. Results will be validated against the Moser/Kim/Moin 1993 DNS data.

Discussion

Periodic hills exercise the solver's ability to handle: (1) curved no-slip boundaries, (2) flow separation and reattachment, (3) turbulent transition, and (4) LES turbulence modeling. The sinusoidal profile is smooth, avoiding sharp-edge singularities, and the flow is driven with periodic x-boundaries plus a streamwise body force (or moving lid) to sustain the channel flow.
