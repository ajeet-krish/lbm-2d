Rotating Cylinder: Magnus Effect

Flow past a spinning cylinder at Re=100. The Magnus effect creates a transverse force proportional to angular velocity. Variable rotation rate (0.5, 1.0, 2.0 rad/ts) demonstrates the lift enhancement with increasing spin. The rotation breaks the symmetry of vortex shedding and deflects the wake.

Discussion

The rotating cylinder case exercises the solver's ability to apply tangential velocity boundary conditions on curved surfaces. The Magnus effect produces a transverse lift force perpendicular to the freestream, proportional to the rotation rate. As the angular velocity increases, the lift coefficient grows while the drag may decrease due to wake suppression on the spinning side.

At sufficiently high rotation rates, vortex shedding can be completely suppressed and the flow becomes steady with a deflected wake. The ratio of surface velocity to freestream velocity (α = ωD / (2U)) governs the transition between these regimes. For α > 1, the Flettner rotor regime produces net thrust in certain configurations, which is the basis for Flettner rotor ship propulsion concepts.
