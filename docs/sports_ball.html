Sports-Ball Surface Roughness

A classic demonstration of how surface texture, not just shape, controls drag. A golf ball flies farther than a smooth ball of the same size because its dimples trip the boundary layer into a turbulent, attached state that delays separation and shrinks the wake. This case compares a smooth cylinder against a dimpled cylinder (a ring of small bumps emulating dimples/seams at coarse lattice resolution) at Re = 100.

Beyond sports equipment, the physics underpins riblet films for drag reduction on aircraft and ships, turbulator strips on wind-turbine blades, and the general principle that engineered roughness can be beneficial when it delays separation. At low resolution (DX = 1 lu), the dimples are intentionally crude -- this demonstrates the concept, not a high-fidelity dimple CFD study.

Validation

Configuration Re Computed Cd Wake
Smooth cylinder 100 -- Baseline, wider wake
Dimpled cylinder 100 -- Delayed separation (expected)
Expected trend (golf-ball analogy): the dimpled surface trips the boundary layer, keeping flow attached longer and reducing Cd relative to the smooth cylinder. At Re = 100 the effect is modest, but the mechanism is the same one that makes a dimpled golf ball outperform a smooth one by ~50% in flight distance. Values above populate from the simulation runs.

Discussion

This case extends the solver beyond smooth-body validation into roughness-controlled separation -- a topic with direct industrial relevance (turbulators on turbine blades, riblets on fuselages). It also shows that at coarse lattice resolution, surface features can be represented as discrete obstacle bumps, a useful technique for studying patterned surfaces without sub-grid geometry.
